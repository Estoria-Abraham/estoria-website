Quieter rooms, lower bills, properly flashed.

The window itself is the cheap part of a window job. The flashing, sealing, and foam — the part that decides whether your wall leaks in five years — is the part we obsess over.

Replacement windows installed like the inspector is watching.

Single-pane aluminum windows from the 1970s and 80s are still everywhere in the San Gabriel Valley. They sweat in the winter, leak heat in the summer, and let in every passing motorcycle. A modern dual-pane vinyl window with low-E glass changes the whole feeling of a room — quieter, more even temperature, less dust on the sill.

But we've seen plenty of replacement window jobs go bad. The window itself is fine — it's the install that fails. Skipped flashing tape. Foam blown in carelessly. Stucco patched with a different color. We do it the slow way: nail-fin or block-frame as appropriate, full sill-pan flashing, low-expansion foam in the cavity, butyl tape on the head, and a clean stucco patch keyed into the existing texture and color.

Retrofit vs. nail-fin — which install is right for your house?

A retrofit (block-frame) install slides the new window inside the existing frame, leaving the stucco untouched. It's faster, cheaper, and great when the existing frame is sound. A nail-fin (full-frame) install removes the old window down to the rough opening and re-flashes from scratch. It costs more but is the right call when there's any sign of water intrusion, dry rot, or when you're already painting or re-stuccoing the exterior.

We walk every opening with you and recommend the install method honestly — not whichever is faster for the crew.

Energy efficiency and Title 24

California's Title 24 sets minimum U-factor and SHGC values for replacement windows. We only install products with NFRC-rated stickers that meet or exceed code, and we handle the documentation. Most of the windows we install qualify for utility rebates — we'll point you to the current programs for SoCalGas and Edison.
